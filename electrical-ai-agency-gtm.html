Opener Set the Frame (5 min)
Hey, I'm doing research ‚Äî not selling anything. I spent years as an apprentice before moving into enterprise tech, and I want to understand what's broken in the trades from people who actually run shops. Mind if I ask some direct questions?
  • They relax when you say "not selling anything"

  • Do NOT mention AI in the opener ‚Äî let them bring it up or let it surface naturally
Layer 1 ‚Äî The Visible Pain (10 min)
Q1 Walk me through a typical week during bid season
Walk me through a typical week during bid season. What does your estimating process actually look like day-to-day?
  • Count how many times they mention "manual" or "spreadsheet" ‚Äî high count = opportunity
  • Note which steps they describe first ‚Äî primary pain surfaces early
  • Listen for: "we lose a lot of bids we shouldn't" or "we win bids we shouldn't have"
  • Avoid: jumping to solutions when they describe a problem
Q2 Who does the takeoff when your lead estimator is out?
Who does the takeoff when your lead estimator is out sick or quits? What happens to the knowledge they carry?
  • Estimator dependency = a recurring nightmare for most shops under $10M
  • Strong signal: "We had to pull someone from the field" or "We missed that bid"
  • Even stronger: "We almost lost everything when [name] left"
  • Follow-up: "Have you ever tried to document their process? What happened?"
Q3 Field operations question
When a foreman is done for the day, what does his reporting process look like? How do you know if a job is running over hours?
  • Most will describe: text messages, paper timesheets, or "I call him"
  • Variance discovery lag is the key metric ‚Äî ask "how many days after a problem starts do you find out?"
  • Follow-up: "What was the last job that surprised you on cost? What happened?"
Layer 2 ‚Äî The Dollar Pain (15 min)
PROBE Qualify the cost ‚Äî make it real
Tell me about a job in the last year where the margin was nothing like you expected. What happened?
  • Get a story, not a category. Stories contain real dollar figures.
  • As they tell the story, probe: "What do you estimate that cost you in gross margin?"
  • If they say a specific number, you've found a qualified buyer ‚Äî they've already done the math
  • Follow-up: "Does that happen once a year? Every quarter? More?"
PROBE Quantify estimating errors
If your estimating was 5% more accurate across all bids, what would that mean for your business in a year?
  • This forces them to calculate ROI themselves ‚Äî the most powerful sales move
  • A $5M revenue shop with 10% net margin = $500K net. 5% improvement = $25K/yr minimum. Easy ROI case.
  • Write down their number. Reference it in your follow-up proposal.
  • Don't challenge their number ‚Äî it anchors your value
PROBE Change order leakage
Roughly what percentage of your change orders actually get billed and collected vs. absorbed as extra work?
  • Industry average: contractors absorb 40‚Äì60% of legitimate change orders as goodwill or because they can't document fast enough
  • If they don't know the percentage ‚Äî that's your opening: "That's exactly what I want to help you figure out"
  • Follow-up: "What does it usually take to get a change order approved by the GC?"
Layer 3 ‚Äî The Workaround (10 min)
Why Layer 3 is the Most Valuable

What they've already tried tells you: (1) they've validated the problem is real enough to spend on, (2) existing solutions aren't good enough, and (3) exactly what your product needs to beat. Someone who "tried Procore and gave up" is a warm lead, not a skeptic.

PROBE Map existing tools and failures
What software are you running right now for estimating and project management? And honestly ‚Äî what do you hate about it?
  • Most common answer: Accubid or ConEst for estimating, something else for PM, lots of Excel
  • Any mention of "we stopped using it after 3 months" = product-market fit gap you can fill
  • Follow-up: "What would make you replace it tomorrow if something better existed?"
  • That answer IS your product roadmap
PROBE The budget question (closing the interview)
If a tool could save you [their dollar number from Layer 2] per year, what would you reasonably pay monthly for it?
  • Most small contractors think in terms of $0‚Äì$500/month. That's fine ‚Äî it informs your pricing tier.
  • If they say $1,000+/month unprompted, you're talking to a growth-mode operator ‚Äî qualify harder
  • Final question: "Would you be open to being a design partner? I'd give you early access in exchange for 2 hours/month of feedback calls."
  • Design partners = your first 5 agency clients

Technical Moat
Historical Job Cost RAG

The more job history you ingest per client, the smarter their estimates get. This creates a compounding data moat ‚Äî your AI gets better the longer they use it. Switching means losing that accumulated intelligence.

Key Technical Risk

Electrical plan sheets are complex. Count accuracy from PDFs will start at 70‚Äì80% and improve with fine-tuning. The MVP positions this as "AI assists, estimator approves" ‚Äî not autonomous estimation.
